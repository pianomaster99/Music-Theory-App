# BrainLift: Learning Music Theory Through Competition and Mind-Muscle Connection
## Owners
Tom Liu
## Purpose
The purpose of this BrainLift is to explore how music theory learning apps can be improved by making theory more active, emotional, competitive, and physically connected through singing.
## In Scope
* Music theory learning apps
* Beginner music theory education
* Gamification and competitiveness
* Emotional engagement in learning
* Mind-muscle connection through singing
* Active practice and feedback loops
* Learning theory by doing, not just memorizing
## Out of Scope
* Full ear-training research
* Advanced music theory pedagogy
* Music production tools
* Professional conservatory-level training
* Building a complete curriculum for all of music theory
* Replacing human music teachers
## DOK 4: Spiky Point of View
Current music theory learning apps suck because they teach theory like dead memorization instead of turning it into a competitive, emotional, feedback-rich mind-muscle skill loop where users sing, physically engage with notes, and feel themselves getting better in real time.

## DOK 3: Insights
### Insight 1
Music theory apps fail when they treat theory as information to recognize instead of a skill to perform.
### Insight 2
Singing can turn abstract theory into a mind-muscle connection because the learner has to physically produce the pitch, not just identify it.
### Insight 3
Competition can make music theory emotionally engaging, but only if it rewards improvement, consistency, and mastery instead of only rewarding the highest-scoring users.
### Insight 4
The best music theory app should feel less like a digital textbook and more like a training game where the user repeatedly acts, receives feedback, and improves.
## DOK 2: Knowledge Tree
### Source 1: *Maestoso: An Intelligent Educational Sketching Tool for Learning Music Theory*
#### DOK 1 — Facts
* Music theory helps musicians write, perform, understand, and express music.
* Many music theory tools are limited because they rely too much on memorization or lack useful feedback.
* Maestoso teaches music theory through active sketching practice.
* Maestoso automatically recognizes student input and gives instructor-like feedback.
#### DOK 2 — Summary
Music theory learning should be active. A strong app should not only explain concepts, but should make the user do something with them and receive feedback. This supports designing a music theory app where users practice, answer, sing, compete, and correct mistakes instead of passively reading definitions.
### Source 2: *The Role of Auditory Feedback in the Motor Learning of Music*
#### DOK 1 — Facts
* Musical learning involves audio-motor associations.
* Auditory feedback helps musicians connect sound with action.
* Auditory feedback is especially important for beginners.
* Listening during practice can improve musical performance.
#### DOK 2 — Summary
Music learning is not purely mental. Beginners need feedback between what they hear and what they do. This supports using singing in a music theory app because singing forces the learner to physically connect their body to the concept they are learning.
### Source 3: *The Influences of Emotion on Learning and Memory*
#### DOK 1 — Facts
* Emotion affects attention, learning, memory, reasoning, and problem solving.
* Emotion influences what information learners focus on.
* Emotion can support memory when it helps learners pay attention.
* Emotional design matters in online learning environments.
#### DOK 2 — Summary
Learning is stronger when the learner is emotionally engaged. A competitive music theory game could make theory feel more intense, fun, and memorable. However, the emotion should help the learner focus on improvement instead of creating stress or embarrassment.
### Source 4: *The Role of Gamified Learning Strategies in Students’ Motivation*
#### DOK 1 — Facts
* Gamification can improve student motivation.
* Common gamification tools include points, badges, rankings, and challenges.
* Motivation from gamification can decline over time if the design becomes repetitive.
* Gamification works best when game elements connect to meaningful learning goals.
#### DOK 2 — Summary
Gamification can help music theory feel less boring, but it cannot just be random points and badges. The game mechanics should push users to practice more, improve accuracy, sing better, and master theory concepts over time.
### Source 5: *Leaderboard Design Principles to Enhance Learning and Motivation*
#### DOK 1 — Facts
* Leaderboards can help learners set goals.
* Leaderboards can increase motivation through competition.
* Poor leaderboard design can discourage learners who feel they are always losing.
* Better leaderboards create achievable success through smaller goals and multiple ways to win.
#### DOK 2 — Summary
Competition can improve a music theory app, but the leaderboard must be designed carefully. A single global ranking could discourage beginners. A better system would use friend leaderboards, beginner leagues, personal bests, streaks, and separate competitions for different skills like singing intervals, recognizing chords, or completing theory challenges.
